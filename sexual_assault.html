Understanding Sexual Assault

Learn about consent, prevention, and support for survivors

What is Sexual Assault?

Sexual assault is any unwanted sexual behavior or activity that makes the victim feel uncomfortable, frightened, or threatened. It's important to understand what constitutes sexual assault and how to protect yourself and others.

Types of Sexual Assault

  • Rape
  • Unwanted touching or groping
  • Forced kissing
  • Non-consensual sharing of intimate images
  • Sexual coercion

Understanding Consent

Consent is a crucial concept in understanding sexual assault. Remember:

  • Consent must be freely given, not forced or coerced
  • Consent can be withdrawn at any time
  • Silence or lack of resistance does not imply consent
  • A person who is intoxicated, unconscious, or underage cannot give consent

Consent Scenario

Read the scenario and decide if proper consent was given:

Alex and Sam are at a party. Alex is clearly drunk and can barely stand. Sam, who is sober, suggests they go upstairs to "have some fun." Alex mumbles something that sounds like "okay" and follows Sam upstairs.

Impact on Survivors

Sexual assault can have serious and long-lasting effects on survivors, including:

  • Physical injuries
  • Emotional trauma and PTSD
  • Depression and anxiety
  • Difficulties in relationships
  • Academic or work performance issues

Myths vs. Facts about Sexual Assault

Myth: Sexual assault is usually committed by strangers.

Fact: Most sexual assaults are committed by someone known to the survivor, such as a friend, acquaintance, or partner.

Myth: If a person doesn't fight back, it wasn't really sexual assault.

Fact: Many survivors freeze or comply out of fear. Lack of physical resistance doesn't mean consent was given.

Myth: Sexual assault is caused by uncontrollable sexual urges.

Fact: Sexual assault is about power and control, not sexual desire. Perpetrators make a choice to assault.

Bystander Intervention

As a bystander, you can play a crucial role in preventing sexual assault. Here are some intervention techniques:

  1. Direct: Directly address the situation (e.g., "Hey, leave them alone!")
  2. Distract: Create a distraction to interrupt the situation (e.g., spill a drink, ask for directions)
  3. Delegate: Get help from others (e.g., friends, security, authorities)
  4. Delay: Check in with the person at risk later if you can't act immediately

Bystander Scenario

You're at a party and see your friend Jay being led away by someone they just met. Jay seems very drunk and can barely walk. What would you do?

Prevention and Safety

While it's never the survivor's fault, there are steps you can take to reduce the risk of sexual assault:

  • Trust your instincts and be aware of your surroundings
  • Communicate clearly about your boundaries
  • Look out for your friends and don't leave them alone in vulnerable situations
  • Learn about bystander intervention techniques
  • Avoid excessive alcohol consumption and never leave drinks unattended

Seeking Help

If you or someone you know has experienced sexual assault, it's important to seek help:

  1. Contact emergency services if you're in immediate danger: 000
  2. Call the National Sexual Assault, Domestic Family Violence Counselling Service: 1800 RESPECT (1800 737 732)
  3. Reach out to a trusted adult, school counselor, or healthcare provider
  4. Consider reporting the incident to the police
  5. Remember, it's not your fault and you're not alone
