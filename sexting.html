Understanding Sexting

Learn about the risks, consequences, and emotional aspects of sexting

What is Sexting?

Sexting involves sending, receiving, or forwarding sexually explicit messages, photographs, or images, primarily between mobile phones. While it may seem harmless or fun, sexting can have serious consequences, especially for young people.

Legal Implications in Australia

  • Sending sexual images without consent is illegal and can be considered a form of sexual harassment
  • Once an image is shared, you lose control over who sees it or where it ends up

Risks of Sexting

  • Images can be shared without your consent
  • Potential for cyberbullying and harassment
  • Damage to your reputation and future opportunities
  • Emotional distress and anxiety
  • Vulnerability to sextortion and blackmail

Decision-Making Scenario

Your crush asks you to send them a revealing photo. What do you do?

Emotional Aspects of Sexting

Sexting can have significant emotional impacts:

  • Pressure and anxiety about expectations
  • Feelings of shame or regret after sending images
  • Trust issues if images are shared without consent
  • Impact on self-esteem and body image
  • Stress from the fear of images being discovered

Sexting Decision Framework

Before engaging in sexting, ask yourself these questions:

  1. Do I feel pressured or coerced in any way?
  2. Am I comfortable with this person potentially sharing this image?
  3. How would I feel if my friends or family saw this image?
  4. Could this impact my future education or career opportunities?
  5. Am I aware of the legal implications?
  6. Is there a safer way to express my feelings or desires?

Protecting Yourself

  • Think carefully before sending any intimate images or messages
  • Remember that digital content can last forever and be shared widely
  • Never feel pressured to send sexual content - it's okay to say no
  • Be aware of the legal risks involved
  • If you receive a sext, don't share it with others

If Your Image Is Shared Without Consent

  1. Don't panic - remember it's not your fault
  2. Tell a trusted adult or seek support from a counselor
  3. Report the incident to the eSafety Commissioner
  4. If you're under 18, consider reporting to the police
  5. Ask the person who shared it to delete it immediately
  6. If it's on a social media platform, report it for removal
